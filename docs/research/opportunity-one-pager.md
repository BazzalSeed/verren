# The AI Chief of Staff for Financial Advisors

*A one-page brief for prospective design partners · July 2026*

## The problem: you're the advisor — and the assistant

An advisor's edge is the relationship. But look at the calendar: **barely 4 hours of a
10-hour day** are spent with clients ([Kitces Research](https://www.kitces.com/)). The rest
is the job nobody hired you for — meeting prep, notes, follow-ups, chasing your own promises.
Across a 100-client book that's **1,350 hours a year** of admin: two-thirds of a full-time
hire, done by the most expensive person in the firm.

The big teams solved this with people — associates, a chief of staff, someone whose whole
job is knowing everything and handling everything. Most advisors get a CRM instead: a filing
cabinet that remembers nothing you don't type and does nothing you don't click.

Advisors are ready for the fix. AI notetakers went from zero to half the industry in one
year (T3 2026) — but a notetaker only writes things down. **Nobody has given the independent
advisor what the mega-teams actually have: someone who remembers everything and handles
everything.**

## Our solution: a chief of staff, not another system to feed

**1. It knows your book in real time — and tells you what matters.** Email, meetings, and
notes flow in; AI keeps every profile current — household changes, goals, concerns,
commitments — with zero data entry. But current data isn't the point; what it *unlocks* is:
walk into every meeting (client or prospect) fully briefed, and get surfaced what you'd
otherwise miss — a job change, an inheritance mention, an RMD coming due, a good client gone
quiet. Deeper knowledge is how advisors win wallet share, referrals, and retention — and a
book with clean, living data is worth more on the day you sell the practice.

**2. It handles the follow-through — with your approval.** Like a great chief of staff, it
doesn't wait to be asked:
- **Books the follow-up meeting** the moment one is promised
- **Drafts the follow-up email** in your voice, with the meeting's commitments already in it
- **Runs your outreach** — birthdays, RMD deadlines, review-cycle touchpoints, clients who've
  gone quiet
- **Preps every meeting** — agenda, open items, what's changed since you last spoke

And like a great chief of staff, it never acts over your head: every action shows you exactly
what it's about to do and executes only on your explicit approval, with a full audit trail.
You stay the advisor; it does everything else. That's hours per client per year returned to
the relationship — the support-staff leverage of a mega-team, at software cost.

**3. Trust, engineered in — not bolted on.** Your clients' financial lives are not training
data. We run our own AI models on infrastructure we control — **client data is never sent to
OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, or any third-party AI provider.** No training on your data, and a
complete audit trail of every action the AI takes, designed for the world the SEC's exam
priorities and FINRA's 2026 AI guidance are creating. When your CCO asks "where does client
data go?", you'll have the best answer in the industry.

## Why now

- The AI wave in advisor tech is cresting: Jump ($80M raised), Zocks ($45M), and Nevis ($40M
  from Sequoia/ICONIQ) prove demand — but they either stop at meeting notes or sell
  top-down to the largest firms. Nobody is building the trusted, always-on right hand for
  the independent advisor — that seat is still open.
- The economics are enormous: **~$31T in US retail advisor-managed assets** generating a
  **~$250–300B/yr advisory fee pool** (Cerulli; Datos Insights). Wealth platforms monetize in
  basis points on assets served (Envestnet, Orion, Addepar) — a platform that returns most of
  an advisor's admin time earns its place in that stack.
- Regulators just made AI governance a buying criterion. A trust-first architecture isn't a
  feature — it's the moat.

| Who | What they are | Why the seat is still open |

Everyone above validates a piece of the thesis. No one combines the three things this
market actually requires: **a living profile of the whole relationship, execution that is
visible and approval-gated, and AI the compliance officer can sign off on** — running on
models we own, priced so an independent advisor can start today.

## Where this goes

We're not asking anyone to rip out their CRM — a chief of staff doesn't make you fire your
filing system; they make it irrelevant. The arc:

1. **Today — the chief of staff.** Connects to email and calendar, syncs with the CRM you
   already run, builds the living profile alongside it, and starts doing the work. Nothing
   to migrate, nothing to bet the practice on — you can say yes in an afternoon.
2. **Then — the record.** As the living profile becomes the most current, most complete
   version of your book, it quietly becomes the version you trust. When advisors stop
   opening the old system, retiring it is their idea — not our requirement.
3. **Eventually — the operating platform for the practice.** The place where client
   knowledge, work, and compliance evidence live; priced the way wealth platforms are
   priced — in basis points on the assets it serves, because it earns a share of the
   capacity it creates. The most trusted seat in the advisor's stack, held by the platform
   that never sent their clients' data anywhere.
